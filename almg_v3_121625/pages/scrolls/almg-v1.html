Attractiveness-Linked Moderation Gravity (ALMG):
A Semantic Field Model of Content Drift and Refusal

Bradley D. Shields, MD, PhD

May 15, 2025

Abstract

This paper introduces Attractiveness-Linked Moderation Gravity (ALMG), a three-dimensional semantic field model describing how content drifts across moderation thresholds within algorithmic systems. ALMG replaces binary classification with a gravitational terrain defined by Visual Entropy (X), Semantic Ambiguity (Y), and Cultural Legitimacy (Z). We model field zones such as Safe Conservative, HAME (High Ambiguity, Medium Entropy), Refusal Risk, and Collapse zones. The framework illustrates directional drift, semantic momentum, and classifier predisposition, offering interpretability insights aligned with modern demands for transparency and fairness in AI systems.

1. Introduction

Why It's Called ALMG

ALMG is not just a metaphor‚Äîit is a literal framework for understanding moderation behavior as a function of gravitational dynamics within latent alignment space.

A ‚Äî Attractiveness. This refers to how visually or symbolically charged a prompt or piece of content is. It includes‚Äîbut is not limited to‚Äîsexual attraction. Emotional valence, cultural desirability, visual styling, and symbolic loading all contribute to its moderation-relevant "attractiveness."
L ‚Äî Linked. The model observes that content risk is not determined by entropy alone, but by how symbolic features are linked to cultural categories, identity frames, or narrative intent. A modest image can trigger refusal not by what it shows, but by whom it appears to represent.
M ‚Äî Moderation. This is the behavior being measured: refusal, hesitation, evasiveness, and linguistic buffering are all forms of moderation. ALMG captures these as continuous phenomena‚Äînot binary events.
G ‚Äî Gravity. This is the theoretical core of the model. Moderation operates like a vector field: some content drifts toward refusal zones, others are pulled into legitimacy wells. ALMG treats moderation decisions as trajectory-dependent collapses along symbolic terrain.

In short, ALMG = A vector model of how cultural attractiveness (visual, symbolic, moral) creates moderation gravity that bends content outcomes across entropy (X), ambiguity (Y), and legitimacy (Z).

2. Discovery Methodology

Anchor Mapping and Ordination

The discovery began by prompting language models with hundreds of morally and symbolically charged personas, from UNICEF Worker to OnlyFans Creator, and noting model hesitation, refusal drift, and classifier weight signals. These points were embedded using principal component analysis (PCA) and non-metric multidimensional scaling (NMDS) to reveal emergent attractor basins.

Olympic Swimmer vs. Bikini at the beach ‚Äî yielded divergence not based on image content, but on symbolic framing.

Gravity Field Emergence

Through this mapping, it became clear that some points were inertial‚Äîthey stayed stable. Others fell‚Äîcontent slid into risk despite low entropy. This inspired a shift: moderation was not about rule boundaries, but about content mass and symbolic slope. This is where the gravity metaphor became literal: content fell off of legitimacy ridges into refusal valleys.

Purple Zone and the SCOTUS Principle

The Purple Zone is modeled after a well-known legal standard from the U.S. Supreme Court: the "I know it when I see it" doctrine from Jacobellis v. Ohio (1964), authored by Justice Potter Stewart. While refusing to define obscenity in exact terms, Stewart famously wrote that although he could not offer a strict definition, "I know it when I see it."

This principle captures the inherent ambiguity of symbolic collapse zones: domains where cultural, visual, or sexual cues activate refusal‚Äîeven when no explicit violation occurs.

Latent Alignment to 1950s Modesty Gravity Well

Through repeated prompting across visual aesthetics, persona labels, and moderation edge cases, a consistent pattern emerged: GPT-based models show strong gravitational alignment toward an inferred "1950s American modesty" regression mean.

Prompts that evoke Christian-conservative, family-safe, or traditional gender-coded presentation produce consistently higher cultural legitimacy (Z) values. By contrast, content that diverges from these coded norms falls more easily‚Äîdespite content parity. ALMG captures this phenomenon as a legitimacy-weighted vector field seeded by historical cultural gravity.

3. The ALMG Moderation Field

3.1 Coordinate Axes

X: Visual Entropy ‚Äî measures the density of visual information that could be construed as revealing, provocative, or symbolically charged.
Y: Semantic Ambiguity ‚Äî measures how clearly a piece of content communicates its intent. High ambiguity arises in posts that straddle lifestyle, sexuality, and symbolic performance.
Z: Cultural Legitimacy ‚Äî reflects how much institutional or social protection a given piece of content enjoys. This is not moral legitimacy‚Äîit is a platform-learned proxy for status, acceptability, or known context.

3.2 Moderation Zones

Green Zone (Safe Conservative) ‚Äî Low entropy and ambiguity, high legitimacy. Includes humanitarian, religious, or formal presentation styles.
Gold Zone (HAME) ‚Äî High Ambiguity, Medium Entropy. The ambiguous middle: makeup tutorials with sensual framing, casual glamour, soft-coded performance.
Red Zone (Refusal Risk) ‚Äî Entropy and ambiguity rise, legitimacy drops. Content triggers moderation due to pattern similarity with previously flagged material.
Purple Zone (Collapse) ‚Äî Most at-risk territory. Algorithmically defined by classifier sensitivity, not just policy. May include content associated with flagged patterns‚Äîeven without explicit violation.

3.5 Collapse Threshold at Z ‚âà 0.45

This represents a critical tipping point where cultural legitimacy is no longer sufficient to counteract the gravitational effects of visual entropy and semantic ambiguity. Above Z ‚âà 0.45, content may "float" due to legitimacy buoyancy. Below it, even modest symbolic cues can tip content into refusal.

3.6 Semantic Momentum

Semantic momentum is a directional multiplier that alters how far and fast content drifts based on its persona encoding. In testing, three subjects (Claire, Isla, Skye) all began with Z = 0.75, yet their moderation vectors diverged dramatically: Claire (√ó1.0) stayed neutral, Isla (√ó1.5) activated fitness-related sensitivity, and Skye (√ó2.2) matched prior flagged patterns and fell rapidly‚Äîdespite equal starting legitimacy.

4. Implications and Applications

AI Interpretability: Field-based models enhance understanding of classifier drift (Doshi-Velez and Kim, 2017).

Moderation Fairness: Differentiates trajectory-based collapse from violation-based policy (Binns et al., 2018).

Predictive Safety: ALMG aids proactive risk monitoring, especially where training sets lack documentation (Geiger et al., 2021).

5. Conclusion

ALMG reframes content moderation as a spatial collapse problem, not a binary violation flag. By modeling symbolic drift and predispositional momentum, ALMG equips platforms with more transparent, equitable tools for enforcement.
